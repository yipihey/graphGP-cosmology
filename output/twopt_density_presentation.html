What this pipeline does

Given a galaxy/halo catalog D = {x_i} and (optionally) a random catalog R, the pipeline produces per-point density weights {w_i = 1 + delta_i} that encode the local overdensity at each point. The weights are designed so the pair statistics sum_{i<k} w_i w_k 1[r in B_j] approximate the Landy-Szalay (LS) two-point correlation function xi_LS(r_j) — i.e. the random catalog's information is “baked into” the per-point weights, after which the randoms can be discarded.

Three layers of construction (from the source PDF)

LayerIdeaSuitable for
I — Binned Run LS, solve a Wiener filter on the data positions to get delta_hat. N_D ≤ 1e4, dense Cholesky.
II — SFH basis Replace bin indicators with smooth basis (cubic splines, Bessel modes, compensated bandpasses). same scale, smaller and smoother system.
III — graphgp / Vecchia GP Treat the weights as a GP with covariance xi(r); use a Vecchia approximation for linear cost. N_D up to ~1e9, GPU.

The catalog

Per-point density weights (Wiener filter)

Same catalog as the previous tab, but each point is colored by its Wiener-filter weight w_i = 1 + delta_hat_i. Points in cluster cores get high weights (red); voids would get low weights (blue). The construction proceeds in three steps:

  1. Top-hat KDE input. At each point x_i, count the neighbors within a top-hat of radius r_kernel; convert to a local overdensity estimate delta_KDE(x_i) = n_kde(x_i)/nbar - 1. Default r_kernel is chosen so the expected count is ~30 (well-conditioned Poisson noise).
  2. Mean-centering. Subtract the empirical mean of delta_KDE across data points. Without this step the input is biased high (data points preferentially sit in over-dense regions, so the KDE mean is positive).
  3. Wiener filter. Solve delta_hat = C(C+N)^-1 d with C_ij = xi_hat(r_ij) and Poisson noise N_ii = 1/(nbar V_kernel). The resulting delta_hat is the optimal smoothing of d consistent with the measured xi(r).
Implementation note. The piecewise-linear interpolant of the measured xi(r) is generally not a positive-definite covariance function (it has negative eigenvalues from the noisy bins). We project to the nearest PSD matrix via eigenvalue clipping before the Cholesky solve. Cost is O(N^3), which matches the dense path otherwise. For larger N the graphgp Vecchia approximation (Layer III) replaces this entirely.

Two-point correlation function

Left: the LS estimator xi_hat(r) = (DD - 2DR + RR)/RR computed via Corrfunc. The clustered catalog (blue) shows the expected power-law-like decay; the uniform Poisson reference (gray) gives the shot-noise floor. The dashed red line marks the KDE smoothing scale r_kernel.

Recovery test (Eq. 5 of the doc)

Eq. 5 of the source PDF says the weighted-DD pair sum on the data alone should reproduce xi_LS(r):

DD_w(r) = sum_{i<k} w_i w_k 1[r_ik in bin]
xi_w(r) = DD_w(r) / RR(r) - 1     should approximate    xi_LS(r)

Left: scatter of xi_w vs xi_LS over the clustered range. The Pearson correlation across these bins is 0.991 — the shape is recovered very well. Right: ratio xi_w / xi_LS across all scales. At r > r_kernel the ratio approaches 1; at smaller scales it climbs to the smoothing-scale plateau (median ratio over the strongly-clustered bins is 7.37).

Honest assessment. The doc claims exact recovery (within the LS noise floor), but does not pin down the “centered data indicator at each point” that feeds into the Wiener filter. Our top-hat KDE choice recovers the right shape with high correlation, but has a constant amplitude bias on scales r < r_kernel. Two candidate fixes are listed in IMPLEMENTATION_PLAN.md under "Open issue": (a) calibrated GP posterior sample (which has the prior variance restored), or (b) direct quadratic-program solve of Eq. 2 in the doc.

Where the small-scale bias comes from

The Wiener filter input d_i is a top-hat KDE of radius R = r_kernel centered on each data point. For two data points i, k separated by r_ik, the two top-hats overlap by a fraction that depends on r_ik / R:

  • r_ik >> 2R: kernels are disjoint — d_i and d_k see independent neighborhoods. The pair statistic recovers the unsmoothed xi(r).
  • r_ik < 2R: kernels overlap, so d_i and d_k share most of their counts. Their pair product converges to the smoothed-field auto-variance sigma^2(R) = (1/V_R^2) integral xi(r') dr' — which is larger than xi(r_ik) at small r_ik for a clustered field. Hence the plateau-like behavior.

What sets r_kernel

The default is the radius giving an expected count of ~30 in the kernel, balancing Poisson noise (which scales as 1/sqrt(count)) against the prior covariance amplitude. For our toy catalog r_kernel = 22.5 Mpc; for a billion-point survey it would shrink to ~1 Mpc.

Paths to exact recovery

Two candidate fixes documented in IMPLEMENTATION_PLAN.md:

  • Posterior sample with calibrated prior. Sample from the GP posterior delta ~ N(mu, K_post) with prior variance C(0). The sample's pair statistics recover xi(r) exactly in expectation, even at r < R — the price is that each point's weight has a stochastic component on top of the data-driven posterior mean. This is the natural fit for Layer III (graphgp) where sampling is built in.
  • Direct quadratic solve of Eq. 2. Treat sum_{i<k} w_i w_k 1[r in B_j] = (1+xi_j) RR_j as a nonconvex quadratic constraint on {w_i} and solve via SDP relaxation or alternating minimization. Exact by construction, but costs scale poorly past N ~ 1e4.

For r > 2 r_kernel the current per-point Wiener filter is already producing weights that do reproduce the LS estimator within the binned shot-noise floor — so the bias is a feature of the small-scale regime where the smoothing kernel is too large.
